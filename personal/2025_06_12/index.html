Home Country Bias

  • Home-country bias is the persistent tendency for investors to allocate a disproportionately large share of their portfolios to securities issued in their own country—far in excess of that country’s weight in the global market-capitalisation universe.
  • Over-exposure to domestic assets: Investors routinely hold 60-90 % of their equity in local stocks even when their domestic market may represent only a fraction of world market value.
  • Driven by familiarity and perceived safety: Comfort with local brands, accounting rules, currency and regulations makes domestic investments feel less risky, reinforcing the bias.
  • Leads to under-diversification: Concentrating in one economy raises portfolio volatility and the risk of country-specific downturns or “lost decades,” which broader global exposure could mitigate.

International diversification – Benefits

International diversification is a double-edged sword: adding foreign equities, bonds or real-assets usually lowers portfolio volatility, broadens return drivers and guards against “lost decades” in any one market, but it also introduces new layers of risk—currency moves, higher costs, unfamiliar regulations and the possibility that political shocks (sanctions, capital-controls or outright market closures) temporarily negate the benefit. A sound policy is therefore to diversify enough to avoid a home-market accident, yet not so far that incremental risks and frictions overwhelm the gain.

  • Trading Halt and Illiquidity: With the suspension of trading, investors were unable to buy or sell shares of ERUS on the open market.

Market-Cap Weighting

  • Market-cap weighting means that the weight of each company in an index fund matches the weight of that company in the market
  • This leads up owning a lot of large-cap stocks, because they make up most of the market
  • There is nothing wrong with capturing market returns by investing in a market-cap weighted index fund

Market beta

  • Market beta is the stock market risk factor
  • A “beta” of 1.0 means your portfolio moves in lock-step with the market; a beta of 0.5 means it moves half as much
  • If the stock market moves by x the portfolio moves by x*beta.

Almost

Market beta (the stock market risk factor) explains about two-thirds of the difference in returns between different portfolios.

E.g. if one portfolio delivered a 10% return and another delivered a 16% return, their relative exposure to market beta would explain about 4% (two-thirds) of that 6% return difference.

Alpha

The idea of a portfolio manager bearing the same amount of risk while delivering a higher return is, of course, desirable. It’s too bad additional evidence has continued to whittle away at the likelihood that much, if any alpha actually exists. As such, alpha is like The Holy Grail of investing: Highly sought-after, but rarely seen.

Three-Factor Model

  • Fama and French proposed a three–factor model:
  • Market risk (market beta)
  • Size (small-cap stocks are riskier than large-cap stocks)
  • Relative price (value stocks are riskier than growth stocks)
32 / 39

I.e they did not simply offer higher returns without any increased risk!

  • The three factor model showed that higher average return from small-cap and value stocks were not a result of active alpha!
  • The model showed that they were exposed to different kinds risk, independent of general market risk
  • The three factor model explains 90% of the difference in return between portfolios

Risk premiums

  • Market beta is the return of the market (a market-cap weighted index), minus the return of one-month Treasury bills (a risk-free asset). From July 1926–December 2018, the S. market premium was 6.28% per year on average.
  • Size is measured by the return of small stocks minus the returns of large stock (SMB, or small minus big). For the same time period, the U.S. size premium was 1.88% per year on average.
  • Value is measured by stocks with a high book value relative to market price, minus stocks with a low book value relative to market price. That’s why it is referred to in the research as HML, or high minus low. For the same time period, the U.S. value premium was 78% per year on average.
