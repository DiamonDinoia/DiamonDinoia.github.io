Why not 100% stocks?

Bonds offer stability, income, and diversification, making them a core component of many portfolios — especially for risk-conscious investors or those nearing retirement.

Bonds

A bond is a type of loan from an investor to a borrower — typically a corporation, municipality, or government. In return, the issuer agrees to:

  • Pay periodic interest (the coupon), and
  • Repay the principal (the original loan, or face/par value) at a specified future date (maturity).

A bond is a fixed-income instrument.

Issuers

  • Governments: US Treasuries, German Bunds
  • Municipal: e.g. NY MTA
  • Corporate: any company

Maturity

  • Short-term: < 3 years
  • Medium-term: 3–10 years
  • Long-term: 10+ years
  • Perpetual: no maturity (rare; some bank instruments)

Equities

Equities represent ownership in a company. When you own equity, you own a piece of the business and are entitled to a share of its profits (typically as dividends) and to potential capital gains if the company’s value increases.

Equities offer strong long-term growth

  • Historical data shows equities outperform bonds and cash over long horizons
  • Investors are compensated for taking on more volatility and risk
  • Growth potential makes equities a core part of most portfolios
  • Reads
  • Fama, E. F. (1965). The Behavior of Stock-Market Prices.
  • Markowitz, H. (1952). Portfolio Selection.

Diversification enhances risk-adjusted returns

  • Combining uncorrelated assets reduces portfolio risk
  • Enables more consistent performance even in downturns
  • Foundation of Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)
  • Reads
  • Markowitz, H. (1952). Portfolio Selection.

Passive investing has proven reliable

  • Index funds consistently outperform most active managers after fees
  • Low-cost, diversified exposure to the entire market
  • Ideal for long-term investors seeking consistent participation in equity growth
  • Reads
  • Wigglesworth, R. (2021). Trillions.
