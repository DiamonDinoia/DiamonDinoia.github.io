Why indexing won — the steel-man

Before the critique, the case must be stated honestly. Bogle's argument has held up for fifty years.

  • Costs: total-market index funds at 3 bps versus active mutual funds at 50–100 bps compound into a multi-decade gap.
  • Tax efficiency: low turnover means few capital-gains distributions.
  • Diversification: thousands of names, no single-stock idiosyncratic risk.
  • Empirics: SPIVA scorecards, year after year, ~85% of active large-cap managers underperform their benchmark over 10 years.

Inelastic markets

If you put $1 into the stock market, how much market cap is created?

Naively: $1. The Gabaix–Koijen "Inelastic Markets Hypothesis" estimates the answer is closer to $5 — because the marginal seller of equity is rare. Demand curves are steep; flows move prices.

Implication: a forced buyer (an index fund taking inflows) does not just rebalance prices, it raises them.

SourceGabaix & Koijen ·NBER w28967 — In Search of the Origins of Financial Fluctuations: The Inelastic Markets Hypothesis

The primary-market channel

Index-fund demand is usually framed as a secondary-market story — index funds buying already-listed shares from other investors. Murray & Sammon (2026) show that the demand also reaches the primary market — the moment new shares are first issued.

  • Primary vs. secondary, briefly
  • Primary market: the company itself sells newly created shares for cash — IPOs (initial public offerings), SEOs (seasoned equity offerings, i.e. follow-on sales by an already-public company), and direct follow-ons.
  • Underwriters: investment banks (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPM…) that price the new shares, line up institutional buyers, and — in a traditional IPO — temporarily buy the shares from the company before reselling them to the market.
  • "The same channel" — what I mean
  • Steady passive inflows (401(k) contributions, target-date funds, ETF buys) → forced buying of every index member → demand that is largely price-insensitive. That is the channel that has compounded the Mag 7's weight in the S&P 500.
  • The moment a newly-public company is admitted to an index, that same price-insensitive demand turns on for its shares — at whatever price the underwriters and early holders chose to set. The flow does not check valuation.
  • Issuers and underwriters know this and time issuance to passive demand. Index inclusion becomes a deliberate exit window, not a side effect.
SourceMurray & Sammon (2026) · Review of Asset Pricing Studies raaf013 ·SSRN: Primary Capital Market Transactions and Index Funds

Indexes time the market — badly

At reconstitution dates, passive funds buy stocks that have just risen and sell stocks that have just fallen. Sammon & Shim (2026) put a number on it.

Drag — high estimate70 bps/yr

Range: 47–70 bps per year in foregone return — the cost of buying high and selling low at index reconstitutions, measured across major US benchmarks.

The mega-IPO pipeline

Concentration plus a forced primary-market channel raises one question: what's coming through it next?

If SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic listed at reported full valuations, their combined weight in the S&P World Index would be ~2.9% — almost as much as Canada.

MSCI's scenario analysis: at a 5% free-float threshold, only 4 of 10 large privates would qualify; at 25% the dollar flows into broad benchmarks become structurally significant.

Impact on the Nasdaq-100

NDX ≈ $32T; smaller and more concentrated than the S&P 500 — top-7 already ~50%. The May 2026 free-float rule changes the size of the inclusion shock dramatically.

  • SpaceX at full cap would land as a top-3 weight (~4.7%); combined three privates add ~7.4 pp.
  • Under the new 3× free-float cap: if SpaceX lists 5% float, its weighting cap collapses from $1.5T to $225B — about 0.7% instead of 4.7%. Same company, very different forced-buy flow.

Why a company IPOs — and why later

  • Two motives, often confused
  • Capital raise: the company sells primary shares for cash to fund operations or capex.
  • Liquidity for insiders: existing equity holders — founders, employees, VCs — sell secondary shares.
  • The trend: companies are public later
  • Median age at IPO: ~4 yrs (1980–2000) → ~13.5 yrs (2024). The 1999–2000 dot-com bubble is the visible exception — median age dipped to ~3 yrs and unprofitable software companies listed within months of founding.
  • Three structural shifts since 2000 made staying private the default:
  • More late-stage private capital. SoftBank Vision Fund, Tiger Global, Coatue, sovereign wealth funds — multi-billion-dollar checks that didn't exist in the 1990s now do the job a public listing used to do.
  • Private credit, ~$2T. Direct lenders (Ares, Blackstone, Apollo) provide debt without the disclosure or covenants public bond markets demand. Companies can lever up while staying private.
  • Secondary liquidity without listing. Tender offers, Forge / EquityZen / Hiive marketplaces, and SPV wrappers let employees and early VCs cash out without the company filing an S-1.
  • Higher cost of being public. Sarbanes-Oxley (2002) and Dodd-Frank disclosure raised the fixed cost of a US listing; quarterly-earnings short-termism and activist exposure raised the soft cost.
  • Consequence
  • By the time the public sees an S-1, most of the value creation has already accrued to private investors. The IPO is increasingly an exit for early holders, not a financing event for the company.
SourcesRitter · IPO Data · Mar 9, 2026 ·University of Florida — IPO Data·BIS & IMF on private credit growth (2024–2025)

Mechanics — the standard book-built IPO

  • 1. Underwriters. The company hires a lead investment bank (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, JPM…) and a syndicate. In a "firm-commitment" IPO, they buy the new shares from the company and resell them to investors.
  • 2. S-1 filing. The company files audited financials and risk factors with the SEC; the public can read them.
  • 3. Roadshow. Management presents to institutional investors (mutual funds, pensions, hedge funds) over ~2 weeks.
  • 4. Book-building & price discovery. Underwriters collect non-binding indications of interest at various prices, build a "book," and the night before listing set an offer price calibrated to clear the book with modest excess demand.
  • 5. Allocation. Shares are handed to favoured institutional clients at the offer price — retail rarely gets in at this stage.
  • 6. First trade. The stock opens on the exchange. A first-day "pop" of 15–30% is the norm — the gap between offer and opening price is value left on the table by the issuing company.
  • 7. Lock-up. 180 days standard, 90 for some recent issues; insiders cannot sell during the window.
  • 8. Staggered selling & follow-ons. After lock-up, insider shares dribble out in tranches; the company can issue more shares ("follow-on offerings"). Each release adds supply that weighs on the price.
SourceSEC — Going Public

Exceptions to the standard path

  • Direct listing
  • Spotify (2018), Slack (2019), Coinbase (2021): no underwriter book-building, no new shares issued. Existing shares simply start trading; the opening price is set entirely by the market on day one.
  • Cuts the underwriter fee and the first-day pop, but only works for companies that don't need to raise primary capital and already have name recognition.
  • Tender offer — the modern substitute for staying private
  • The company stays private. Specific buyers purchase existing shares from employees and early investors at a negotiated price.
  • Establishes a private mark without filing an S-1 — no public disclosure, no quarterly earnings, no Sarbanes-Oxley overhead.
  • Stripe priced a $159B tender in Feb 2026; SpaceX runs biannual tenders.
SourcesStripe Newsroom · Feb 24, 2026·SEC — direct listing rules

Index inclusion — the 2026 changes

Nasdaq-100 (NDX)

  • Fast-entry rule effective May 1, 2026.
  • Previously: securities with <10% public float excluded outright.
  • New rule: no minimum float, but <20% float gets weight reduced — the cap used for index weighting is set to 3× the value of the publicly-floated shares, not the full company market cap.
  • Example: $1T company, 10% public float ($100B floated). Weight is computed as if the cap were 3 × $100B = $300B — a 70% cut versus the full valuation.
  • Whole-company market cap (listed + non-listed shares) is also factored into eligibility checks.

CRSP US Total Market (VTI)

  • Admits qualifying IPOs in ~5 trading days.
  • Means VTI takes mega-IPOs faster than SPY.

S&P 500

  • Still: 12-month seasoning, four quarters GAAP profitability.
  • Multi-class share ban (2017) partially reopened in April 2023; S&P retains discretion.
  • Bloomberg (Mar 12, 2026): S&P weighing rule changes that would speed SpaceX's S&P 500 entry.
SourcesNasdaq NDX Consultation · Feb 2026·CRSP Methodology Guide · Jan 2026·SPDJI Methodology · Feb 2026·Almeida, Porter & Gould · Bloomberg · Mar 12, 2026·Davis Polk · 2023 multi-class reopening

Valuation problem

We use price-to-sales (P/S = market cap ÷ trailing revenue), not price-to-earnings. P/E is undefined when earnings are negative and erratic when they are thin or lumpy — exactly the regime SpaceX, Palantir-at-IPO, and most pre-IPO mega-caps live in. Revenue is positive, smoother, and harder to manipulate, so P/S is the only multiple that puts everyone on the same axis.

SpaceX at the reported IPO range trades at ~94× sales. For comparison, the S&P 500 sits near 3.1×; Facebook went public at ~11× and Google at ~5–6×.

SourcesPublic filings (Palantir, Meta S-1, Google S-1) ·SPDJI · Apr 2026 ·Felix / PWL Capital commentary

The New Issues Puzzle

Loughran & Ritter (1995): IPOs from 1970–1990 returned ~5% / yr versus ~12% for size-matched seasoned firms — investors needed 44% more capital to retire on the same number of shares.

  • Renaissance US IPO ETF (IPO) since Oct 2013: VTI +376%, IPO +159% — a ~5.4 pp/yr annualised gap.
  • Green & Black (Dimensional, 2019): ~2 pp/yr underperformance 1991–2018.

The shadow tax — and the Tesla precedent

When a stock is announced for index inclusion, hedge funds front-run the forced buying. Fast-tracked index entrants outperform non-fast-track by ~5 pp peaking at the inclusion date, then revert.

Tesla joined the S&P 500 on Dec 21, 2020. Since then, SPY +105% vs TSLA +60% — TSLA has underperformed the index it joined by ~45 pp cumulative. The first-day-of-inclusion price was the front-run peak.

A personal playbook

You can't change index methodology. You can shape your exposure.

  • Four levers
  • Lever 1. Tilt away from cap-weighted concentration with evidence-based factor funds.
  • Lever 2. Avoid the obvious traps — pre-IPO ETFs, SPVs, fund-of-funds private equity.
  • Lever 3. Behave well around inclusion events.
  • Lever 4. What is actually in your control — costs, taxes, contributions, time.

What those funds are actually harvesting

Annualised historical excess returns of the standard academic factor portfolios — what evidence-based funds tilt toward (Part 3 recap):

  • MKT−RF — equity risk premium: stock market return minus the risk-free rate. The base reward for owning equities at all.
  • SMB ("Small Minus Big") — small-cap stocks minus large-cap stocks. Small companies have historically paid extra return for the extra risk.
  • HML ("High Minus Low") — value stocks (high book-to-market) minus growth stocks. Cheap stocks have historically beaten expensive ones.
  • RMW ("Robust Minus Weak") — highly profitable companies minus weakly profitable ones. Quality compounds.

Hiding in plain sight

Before deciding what private-market vehicles to avoid, the premise of the pitch is worth checking. The claim — "you have no AI or space exposure unless you own SpaceX" — is empirically false.

Most "private-asset themes" sold to retail investors are already available through public markets, more cheaply and with daily liquidity.

  • Where the exposure already lives
  • AI compute — NVDA, AMD, AVGO, TSM, ASML. The picks-and-shovels of every private AI lab are public; OpenAI's compute spend ultimately flows to these names' P&Ls.
  • Space & defense — LMT, RTX, NOC, BA cover launch, satellites, and prime contracts; RKLB and LUNR are public small-cap pure-plays.
  • SaaS / growth — public small- and mid-cap value and quality factor funds already span the same business models VCs sell as "exclusive."
  • Why the public route is usually better
  • Daily liquidity, transparent NAV, audited financials, lower headline fees, no SPV layer.
  • Dimensional: when researchers map private-asset return drivers onto public-market factor exposures, most of the variance is captured without paying private-market fees.
SourceAhn, Hendrix & Saito · Dimensional · Jan 2, 2026 ·Hiding in Plain Sight: Private Asset Exposure Through Public Equities

Levers 3 & 4 — Behaviour around inclusion

  • Don't trade the inclusion event. It has already been front-run; the shadow tax peaks at inclusion and reverts.
  • Don't sell on a single mega-cap drawdown. The Sammon–Shim drag is minimised by holding through.
  • The mega-IPO is an emotional trap: famous company, small float, high price, forced index buyer. Your job is to do nothing.

"The more you covet something, the more you should probably question your desire to own it." — Jeff Ptak, Morningstar

One-line summary. Pick the right rules-based fund (DFA / Avantis / total-market). Avoid IPO ETFs and SPVs. Sit still during inclusion drama.

SourcePtak · Morningstar · 2026

The loop, restated

  1. Passive flows raise mega-cap valuations.
  2. 2026 rule changes (NDX fast-entry, CRSP 5-day, S&P 500 review) accelerate the inclusion of new mega-caps.
  3. Primary issuance lands directly into indexes via the Murray–Sammon channel.
  4. Forced buyers absorb low-float IPOs at inclusion prices.
  5. The underperformance shows up as a slow drag — 47–70 bps/yr at the index level, much more at the individual-issue level.

SpaceX is the test case at $1.5–1.75T. The personal playbook (slides 22–25) is what an individual investor can actually control.
